Hello world!

19 | February | 2018

Hiya, I’m Lou, full stack JavaScript developer in the making. And now blogger too, it seems. Lucky world.

While I’m still levelling up to become a kick-ass JavaScript developer, I’m an associate lecturer at the University of York. I develop study skills modules for international students, coordinate teams of tutors, and do the odd bit of teaching. I’ve also worked in universities in Japan and Vietnam; similar roles, but much better food (imho, Vietnamese food > Japanese food. Please don't tell my friends in Japan).

I was also a speech science researcher for a couple of years at UCL, where I did an MPhil investigating how people understand variation in accents. I didn’t make any ground-breaking discoveries, but I did discover coding. Or rather, I was tossed into the deep, murky sea of coding without a lifeguard present. After a pretty steep learning curve with R, MATLAB and a bit of a dabbling with Python, some tears, a couple of light bulb moments and a lot of pretty ggplot2 graphs, I was hooked. The magic of being able to turn just a few lines on a text editor into something useful, beautiful, or fun (hopefully all three!) is addictive, and being able to create tools that solve problems and save tons of time makes me feel like a wizard. An efficient, nerdy wizard.

So why am I now working towards becoming a full stack JS developer instead of pursuing my love of ggplot2's oh so shiny graphs? A difference of priorities now that I'm no longer an academic researcher.
The Dataist puts it nicely:

Data scientists write code as a means to an end, whereas ... developers write code to build things.
After what I've learned since my time as a researcher, I much prefer using code to build things rather than code being an analytical tool. Also, I've found JavaScript to be much more accessible than R. Win win.

Lastly, why bother writing about becoming a kick-ass JavaScript developer, seeing as there’s so many learn to code blogs already? Mostly selfish on this one - writing about my experiences will help me reflect on and consolidate what I’m learning. I can track my progress, and future me can giggle at past me’s coding efforts. It’s not all selfish though - maybe sharing my experiences can help some other people out. Saying that, I expect that mum will probably be my main reader (hi mum!).
